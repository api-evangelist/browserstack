<h3 id="youve-been-building-software-since-1999-and-moved-from-qa-to-ceo-of-test-scouts-ab-how-has-your-definition-of-quality-evolved">You&apos;ve been building software since 1999 and moved from QA to CEO of Test Scouts AB. How has your definition of &quot;quality&quot; evolved?</h3><p>I actually started as a developer and did that for a decade before moving into testing. And to be honest, I don&apos;t think it&apos;s changed much. </p><p>I&apos;ve always been very interested in quality from requirement creation to working in production &#x2014; that&apos;s just how I was schooled. It likely shaped how I&apos;ve worked with software in all of my roles and is also the foundation to why I always struggled to understand why other people saw testing as something limiting and small.</p><p>For me, quality starts with the idea and ends when software is decommissioned. The code is just a tiny part :-)</p><h3 id="can-you-give-us-a-sneak-peek-into-your-breakpoint-session-whats-the-core-message-you-want-to-deliver">Can you give us a sneak peek into your Breakpoint session? What&apos;s the core message you want to deliver?</h3><p>I will talk about something I find absolutely fascinating: how incredibly difficult it is to write clear, understandable messages of different kinds. Error messages, information messages, warnings, popups. </p><p>And it LOOKS so simple, and is so funny when it goes wrong. Not to mention the fascinating differences we can see historically and how much you can learn about architecture and systems by studying them.</p><blockquote>Most teams treat system messages as an afterthought. But they&#x2019;re often the first place users experience failure &#x2014; and the only clue teams have when something breaks.</blockquote>
